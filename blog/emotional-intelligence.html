What is Emotional Intelligence?

One definition (from Saolvey and Mayer 1990, as per our notes) is that it is: the ability to monitor one's own and others feelings and emotions, to discriminate among them and to use this information to guide one's thinking and actions.

There are three steps to Emotional Intelligence:

  1. The practice of mindfulness
  2. Self-knowledge and mastery
    • E.g. of who we are, how we are and how we engage in any particular situation
    • From this comes mastery ... you know what happens when you get in certain situations and how to regulate it
  3. Creating useful mental habits
    • Use as defences against negative, critical thoughts that you might have.
    • Train our minds to develop habits to defend against the 'superego' (the critical, judging mind).

How is it different to IQ?

IQ usually refers to your intellectual ability, including how well you can:

  • Use logic
  • Solve problems
  • Plan, strategise
  • Understand abstract ideas
  • Learn and adapt to change
  • Grasp and use language

EQ is more to do with your ability to sense emotion in yourself and in other people, and also how you use that awareness to guide your behaviour.

If you have high EQ, you may find it easier to:

  • Identify emotions in yourself and others
  • Empathise with others
  • Adapt your feelings and behaviours to different situations
  • Control your impulses
  • Withstand temptations
  • Resolve conflicts with others
  • Communicate effectively.

Why is it important?

Three primary benefits to developing EQ are:

  1. Stellar performance
    • According to Daniel Goldman (one of the founders of Emotional Intelligence), studies have shown EQ is twice as important as IQ in a work environment. It becomes even more important the higher up the ladder you go.
  2. Outstanding leadership
    • Once you get to higher levels, EQ skills account for close to 90% of what distinguishes outstanding leaders from average ones (Kemper 1999).
  3. Happiness
    • Strive for an "optimal state of being", not just a pleasurable feeling or a fleeting mood or emotion.

Emotional intelligence has been linked to job success and more satisfying relationships. There's also evidence that emotional intelligence may help you handle stress. A 2019 research review concluded that emotional intelligence can help you recover more quickly from acute stress.
